Section 5.3Double Integrals in Polar Coordinates

Double integrals are sometimes much easier to evaluate if we change rectangular coordinates to polar coordinates. However, before we describe how to make this change, we need to establish the concept of a double integral in a polar rectangular region.

Polar Rectangular Regions of Integration

When we defined the double integral for a continuous function in rectangular coordinates—say, \(g\) over a region \(R\) in the \(xy\) -plane—we divided \(R\) into subrectangles with sides parallel to the coordinate axes. These sides have either constant \(x\) -values and/or constant \(y\) -values. In polar coordinates, the shape we work with is a polar rectangle, whose sides have constant \(r\) -values and/or constant \(θ\) -values. This means we can describe a polar rectangle as in Figure 1(a), with \(R=\{(r,θ)|a\le r\le b,α\le θ\le β\}.\)

In this section, we are looking to integrate over polar rectangles. Consider a function \(f(r,θ)\) over a polar rectangle \(R.\) We divide the interval \([a,b]\) into \(m\) subintervals \([{r}_{i-1},{r}_{i}]\) of length \(\Delta r=(b-a)\text{/}m\) and divide the interval \([α,β]\) into \(n\) subintervals \([{θ}_{j-1},{θ}_{j}]\) of width \(\Delta θ=(β-α)\text{/}n.\) This means that the circles \(r={r}_{i}\) and rays \(θ={θ}_{j}\) for \(1\le i\le m\) and \(1\le j\le n\) divide the polar rectangle \(R\) into smaller polar subrectangles \({R}_{ij}\) (Figure 1(b)).

As before, we need to find the area \(\Delta A\) of the polar subrectangle \({R}_{ij}\) and the “polar” volume of the thin box above \({R}_{ij}.\) Recall that, in a circle of radius \(r,\) the length \(s\) of an arc subtended by a central angle of \(θ\) radians is \(s=rθ.\) Notice that the polar rectangle \({R}_{ij}\) looks a lot like a trapezoid with parallel sides \({r}_{i-1}\Delta θ\) and \({r}_{i}\Delta θ\) and with a width \(\Delta r.\) Hence the area of the polar subrectangle \({R}_{ij}\) is

\[\Delta A=\frac{1}{2}\Delta r({r}_{i-1}\Delta θ+{r}_{i}\Delta θ).\]

Simplifying and letting \({r}_{ij}^{*}=\frac{1}{2}({r}_{i-1}+{r}_{i}),\) we have \(\Delta A={r}_{ij}^{*}\Delta r\Delta θ.\) Therefore, the polar volume of the thin box above \({R}_{ij}\) (Figure 2) is

\[f({r}_{ij}^{*},{θ}_{ij}^{*})\Delta A=f({r}_{ij}^{*},{θ}_{ij}^{*}){r}_{ij}^{*}\Delta r\Delta θ.\]

Using the same idea for all the subrectangles and summing the volumes of the rectangular boxes, we obtain a double Riemann sum as

\[∑i=1m∑j=1nf({r}_{ij}^{*},{θ}_{ij}^{*}){r}_{ij}^{*}\Delta r\Delta θ.\]

As we have seen before, we obtain a better approximation to the polar volume of the solid above the region \(R\) when we let \(m\) and \(n\) become larger. Hence, we define the polar volume as the limit of the double Riemann sum,

\[V=\underset{m,n\to \infty }{\text{lim}}∑i=1m∑j=1nf({r}_{ij}^{*},{θ}_{ij}^{*}){r}_{ij}^{*}\Delta r\Delta θ.\]

This becomes the expression for the double integral.

Definition

The double integral of the function \(f(r,θ)\) over the polar rectangular region \(R\) in the \(rθ\) -plane is defined as

\[\underset{R}{∬}f(r,θ)dA=\underset{m,n\to \infty }{\text{lim}}∑i=1m∑j=1nf({r}_{ij}^{*},{θ}_{ij}^{*})\Delta A=\underset{m,n\to \infty }{\text{lim}}∑i=1m∑j=1nf({r}_{ij}^{*},{θ}_{ij}^{*}){r}_{ij}^{*}\Delta r\Delta θ.\]

Again, just as in Double Integrals over Rectangular Regions, the double integral over a polar rectangular region can be expressed as an iterated integral in polar coordinates. Hence,

\[\underset{R}{∬}f(r,θ)dA=\underset{R}{∬}f(r,θ)r\,dr\,dθ=∫θ=αθ=β\,∫r=ar=bf(r,θ)r\,dr\,dθ.\]

Notice that the expression for \(dA\) is replaced by \(r\,dr\,dθ\) when working in polar coordinates. Another way to look at the polar double integral is to change the double integral in rectangular coordinates by substitution. When the function \(f\) is given in terms of \(x\) and \(y,\) using \(x=r\,\text{cos}\,θ,y=r\,\text{sin}\,θ,\,\text{and}\,dA=r\,dr\,dθ\) changes it to

\[\underset{R}{∬}f(x,y)dA=\underset{R}{∬}f(r\,\text{cos}\,θ,r\,\text{sin}\,θ)r\,dr\,dθ.\]

Note that all the properties listed in Double Integrals over Rectangular Regions for the double integral in rectangular coordinates hold true for the double integral in polar coordinates as well, so we can use them without hesitation.

General Polar Regions of Integration

To evaluate the double integral of a continuous function by iterated integrals over general polar regions, we consider two types of regions, analogous to Type I and Type II as discussed for rectangular coordinates in Double Integrals over General Regions. It is more common to write polar equations as \(r=f(θ)\) than \(θ=f(r),\) so we describe a general polar region as \(D=\{(r,θ)|α\le θ\le β,{h}_{1}(θ)\le r\le {h}_{2}(θ)\}\) (see the following figure).

Double Integrals over General Polar Regions

If \(f(r,θ)\) is continuous on a general polar region \(D\) as described above, then

\[\underset{D}{∬}f(r,θ)r\,dr\,dθ=∫θ=αθ=β\,∫r={h}_{1}(θ)r={h}_{2}(θ)f(r,θ)r\,dr\,dθ\]

Example 5

Evaluate the integral \(\underset{D}{∬}{r}^{2} · sin θ · r\,dr\,dθ\) where \(D\) is the region bounded by the polar axis and the upper half of the cardioid \(r=1+\text{cos}\,θ.\)

Describe the region under the cardioid r=1+cos θ as a general polar region with r running from 0 to 1+cos θ.

We can describe the region \(D\) as \(\{(r,θ)|0\le θ\le \pi ,0\le r\le 1+\text{cos}\,θ\}\) as shown in the following figure.

Hence, we have

\[\begin{array}{ll}\underset{D}{∬}{r}^{2} · sin θ · r\,dr\,dθ & =∫θ=0θ=\pi \,∫r=0r=1+\text{cos }θ{r}^{3} · sin θ\,dr\,dθ \\ & =\frac{1}{4}∫θ=0θ=\pi {[{r}^{4}]}_{r=0}^{r=1+\text{cos}\,θ}\text{sin}\,θ\,dθ \\ & =\frac{1}{4}∫θ=0θ=\pi {(1+\text{cos}\,θ)}^{4}\text{sin}\,θ\,dθ \\ & =-\frac{1}{4}{[\frac{{(1+\text{cos}\,θ)}^{5}}{5}]}_{0}^{\pi }=\frac{8}{5}.\end{array}\]

Polar Areas and Volumes

As in rectangular coordinates, if a solid \(S\) is bounded by the surface \(z=f(r,θ),\) as well as by the surfaces \(r=a,r=b,θ=α,\) and \(θ=β,\) we can find the volume \(V\) of \(S\) by double integration, as

\[V=\underset{R}{∬}f(r,θ)r\,dr\,dθ=∫θ=αθ=β\,∫r=ar=bf(r,θ)r\,dr\,dθ.\]

If the base of the solid can be described as \(D=\{(r,θ)|α\le θ\le β,{h}_{1}(θ)\le r\le {h}_{2}(θ)\},\) then the double integral for the volume becomes

\[V=\underset{D}{∬}f(r,θ)r\,dr\,dθ=∫θ=αθ=β\,∫r={h}_{1}(θ)r={h}_{2}(θ)f(r,θ)r\,dr\,dθ.\]

We illustrate this idea with some examples.

Example 6

Find the volume of the solid that lies under the paraboloid \(z=1-{x}^{2}-{y}^{2}\) and above the unit circle on the \(xy\) -plane (see the following figure).

Recognize this volume integral is the same double integral already evaluated for the unit disk, just reinterpreted geometrically.

By the method of double integration, we can see that the volume is the iterated integral of the form \(\underset{R}{∬}(1-{x}^{2}-{y}^{2})dA\) where \(R=\{(r,θ)|0\le r\le 1,0\le θ\le 2\pi \}.\)

This integration was shown before in Example 3, so the volume is \(\frac{\pi }{2}\) cubic units.

Example 7

Find the volume of the solid that lies under the paraboloid \(z=4-{x}^{2}-{y}^{2}\) and above the disk \({(x-1)}^{2}+{y}^{2}=1\) on the \(xy\) -plane. See the paraboloid in Figure 8 intersecting the cylinder \({(x-1)}^{2}+{y}^{2}=1\) above the \(xy\) -plane.

Convert the shifted circle (x-1)²+y²=1 to polar form to find r=2cos θ as the outer boundary of the region.

First change the disk \({(x-1)}^{2}+{y}^{2}=1\) to polar coordinates. Expanding the square term, we have \({x}^{2}-2x+1+{y}^{2}=1.\) Then simplify to get \({x}^{2}+{y}^{2}=2x,\) which in polar coordinates becomes \({r}^{2}=2r\,\text{cos}\,θ\) and then either \(r=0\) or \(r=2\,\text{cos}\,θ.\) Similarly, the equation of the paraboloid changes to \(z=4-{r}^{2}.\) Therefore we can describe the disk \({(x-1)}^{2}+{y}^{2}=1\) on the \(xy\) -plane as the region

\[D=\{(r,θ)|0\le θ\le \pi ,0\le r\le 2\,\text{cos}\,θ\}.\]

Hence the volume of the solid below the paraboloid \(z=4-{x}^{2}-{y}^{2}\) and above \(r=2\,\text{cos}\,θ\) is

\[\begin{array}{ll}V & =\underset{D}{∬}f(r,θ)r\,dr\,dθ=∫θ=-\frac{\pi }{2}θ=\frac{\pi }{2}\,∫r=0r=2\,\text{cos}\,θ(4-{r}^{2})r\,dr\,dθ \\ & =∫θ=-\frac{\pi }{2}θ=\frac{\pi }{2}[4\frac{{r}^{2}}{2}-{\frac{{r}^{4}}{4}|}_{0}^{2\,\text{cos}\,θ}]dθ \\ & =∫-\frac{\pi }{2}\frac{\pi }{2}[8\,{\text{cos}}^{2}θ-4\,{\text{cos}}^{4}θ]dθ={[\frac{5}{2}θ+\frac{5}{2}\text{sin}\,\text{2}θ-\frac{1}{8}\text{sin}\,\text{4}θ]}_{-\frac{\pi }{2}}^{\frac{\pi }{2}}=\frac{5}{2}\pi .\end{array}\]

Notice in the next example that integration is not always easy with polar coordinates. Complexity of integration depends on the function and also on the region over which we need to perform the integration. If the region has a more natural expression in polar coordinates or if \(f\) has a simpler antiderivative in polar coordinates, then the change in polar coordinates is appropriate; otherwise, use rectangular coordinates.

Example 8

Find the volume of the region that lies under the paraboloid \(z={x}^{2}+{y}^{2}\) and above the triangle enclosed by the lines \(y=x,x=0,\) and \(x+y=2\) in the \(xy\) -plane (Figure 9).

Set up the region and integral in polar coordinates first, notice the resulting integral is messy, then switch to rectangular coordinates instead.

First examine the region over which we need to set up the double integral and the accompanying paraboloid.

The region \(D\) is \(\{(x,y)|0\le x\le 1,x\le y\le 2-x\}.\) Converting the lines \(y=x,x=0,\) and \(x+y=2\) in the \(xy\) -plane to functions of \(r\) and \(θ,\) we have \(θ=\pi \text{/}4,\) \(θ=\pi \text{/}2,\) and \(r=2\text{/}(\text{cos}\,θ+\text{sin}\,θ),\) respectively. Graphing the region on the \(xy\) -plane, we see that it looks like \(D=\{(r,θ)|\pi \text{/}4\le θ\le \pi \text{/}2,0\le r\le 2\text{/}(\text{cos}\,θ+\text{sin}\,θ)\}.\) Now converting the equation of the surface gives \(z={x}^{2}+{y}^{2}={r}^{2}.\) Therefore, the volume of the solid is given by the double integral

\[\begin{array}{ll}V & =\underset{D}{∬}f(r,θ)r\,dr\,dθ=∫θ=\pi \text{/}4θ=\pi \text{/}2\,∫r=0r=2\text{/}(\text{cos}\,θ+\text{sin}\,θ){r}^{2}r\,dr\,dθ={∫\pi \text{/}4\pi \text{/}2[\frac{{r}^{4}}{4}]}_{0}^{2\text{/}(\text{cos}\,θ+\text{sin}\,θ)}dθ \\ & =\frac{1}{4}{∫\pi \text{/}4\pi \text{/}2(\frac{2}{\text{cos}\,θ+\text{sin}\,θ})}^{4}dθ=\frac{16}{4}{∫\pi \text{/}4\pi \text{/}2(\frac{1}{\text{cos}\,θ+\text{sin}\,θ})}^{4}dθ=4{∫\pi \text{/}4\pi \text{/}2(\frac{1}{\text{cos}\,θ+\text{sin}\,θ})}^{4}dθ.\end{array}\]

As you can see, this integral is very complicated. So, we can instead evaluate this double integral in rectangular coordinates as

\[V=∫01\,∫x2-x({x}^{2}+{y}^{2})dy\,dx.\]

Evaluating gives

\[\begin{array}{ll}V & =∫01\,∫x2-x({x}^{2}+{y}^{2})dy\,dx={∫01[{x}^{2}y+\frac{{y}^{3}}{3}]|}_{x}^{2-x}dx \\ & =∫01\frac{8}{3}-4x+4{x}^{2}-\frac{8{x}^{3}}{3}dx \\ & ={[\frac{8x}{3}-2{x}^{2}+\frac{4{x}^{3}}{3}-\frac{2{x}^{4}}{3}]|}_{0}^{1}=\frac{4}{3}.\end{array}\]

To answer the question of how the formulas for the volumes of different standard solids such as a sphere, a cone, or a cylinder are found, we want to demonstrate an example and find the volume of an arbitrary cone.

Example 9

Use polar coordinates to find the volume inside the cone \(z=2-\sqrt{{x}^{2}+{y}^{2}}\) and above the \(xy\text{-plane}\text{.}\)

Find the circular base of the cone by setting z=0, then set up the polar double integral for the height function 2-r.

The region \(D\) for the integration is the base of the cone, which appears to be a circle on the \(xy\text{-plane}\) (see the following figure).

We find an equation of the circle by setting \(z=0\text{:}\)

\[\begin{array}{lll}0 & = & 2-\sqrt{{x}^{2}+{y}^{2}} \\ 2 & = & \sqrt{{x}^{2}+{y}^{2}} \\ {x}^{2}+{y}^{2} & = & 4.\end{array}\]

This means the radius of the circle is \(2,\) so for the integration we have \(0\le θ\le 2\pi\) and \(0\le r\le 2.\) Substituting \(x=r\,\text{cos}\,θ\) and \(y=r\,\text{sin}\,θ\) in the equation \(z=2-\sqrt{{x}^{2}+{y}^{2}}\) we have \(z=2-r.\) Therefore, the volume of the cone is

\(∫θ=0θ=2\pi \,∫r=0r=2(2-r)r\,dr\,dθ=2\pi \frac{4}{3}=\frac{8\pi }{3}\) cubic units.

Analysis

Note that if we were to find the volume of an arbitrary cone with radius \(a\) units and height \(h\) units, then the equation of the cone would be \(z=h-\frac{h}{a}\sqrt{{x}^{2}+{y}^{2}}.\)

We can still use Figure 10 and set up the integral as \(∫θ=0θ=2\pi \,∫r=0r=a(h-\frac{h}{a}r)r\,dr\,dθ.\)

Evaluating the integral, we get \(\frac{1}{3}\pi {a}^{2}h.\)

As with rectangular coordinates, we can also use polar coordinates to find areas of certain regions using a double integral. As before, we need to understand the region whose area we want to compute. Sketching a graph and identifying the region can be helpful to realize the limits of integration. Generally, the area formula in double integration will look like

\[\text{Area}\,A=∫αβ\,∫{h}_{1}(θ){h}_{2}(θ)1r\,dr\,dθ.\]

Example 10

Evaluate the area bounded by the curve \(r=\text{cos}\,4θ.\)

Use symmetry to find the area of a single petal by integrating from θ=-π/8 to π/8, then multiply by 8.

Sketching the graph of the function \(r=\text{cos}\,4θ\) reveals that it is a polar rose with eight petals (see the following figure).

Using symmetry, we can see that we need to find the area of one petal and then multiply it by \(8.\) Notice that the values of \(θ\) for which the graph passes through the origin are the zeros of the function \(\text{cos}\,4θ,\) and these are odd multiples of \(\pi \text{/}8.\) Thus, one of the petals corresponds to the values of \(θ\) in the interval \([\text{-}\pi \text{/}8,\pi \text{/}8].\) Therefore, the area bounded by the curve \(r=\text{cos}\,4θ\) is

\[\begin{array}{ll}A & =8∫θ=\text{-}\pi \text{/}8θ=\pi \text{/}8\,∫r=0r=\text{cos}\,4θ1r\,dr\,dθ \\ & =8∫\text{-}\pi \text{/}8\pi \text{/}8[\frac{1}{2}{{r}^{2}|}_{0}^{\text{cos}\,4θ}]dθ=8∫\text{-}\pi \text{/}8\pi \text{/}8\frac{1}{2}{\text{cos}}^{2}4θ\,dθ=8[\frac{1}{4}θ+{\frac{1}{16}\text{sin}\,4θ\,\text{cos}\,4θ|}_{\text{-}\pi \text{/}8}^{\pi \text{/}8}]=8[\frac{\pi }{16}]=\frac{\pi }{2}.\end{array}\]

Example 11

Find the area enclosed by the circle \(r=3\,\text{cos}\,θ\) and the cardioid \(r=1+\text{cos}\,θ.\)

Find the angle where the circle and cardioid intersect, then split the area into two polar integrals over the two subintervals.

First and foremost, sketch the graphs of the region (Figure 12).

We can from see the symmetry of the graph that we need to find the points of intersection. Setting the two equations equal to each other gives

\[3\,\text{cos}\,θ=1+\text{cos}\,θ.\]

One of the points of intersection is \(θ=\pi \text{/}3.\) The area above the polar axis consists of two parts, with one part defined by the cardioid from \(θ=0\) to \(θ=\pi \text{/}3\) and the other part defined by the circle from \(θ=\pi \text{/}3\) to \(θ=\pi \text{/}2.\) By symmetry, the total area is twice the area above the polar axis. Thus, we have

\[A=2[∫θ=0θ=\pi \text{/}3\,∫r=0r=1+\text{cos}\,θ1r\,dr\,dθ+∫θ=\pi \text{/}3θ=\pi \text{/}2\,∫r=0r=3\,\text{cos}\,θ1r\,dr\,dθ].\]

\[A=2(\frac{1}{4}\pi +\frac{9}{16}\sqrt{3}+\frac{3}{8}\pi -\frac{9}{16}\sqrt{3})=2(\frac{5}{8}\pi )=\frac{5}{4}\pi \,\text{square units}\text{.}\]

Key Concepts

  • To apply a double integral to a situation with circular symmetry, it is often convenient to use a double integral in polar coordinates. We can apply these double integrals over a polar rectangular region or a general polar region, using an iterated integral similar to those used with rectangular double integrals. (See Example 1 and Example 5.)
  • The area \(dA\) in polar coordinates becomes \(r\,dr\,dθ.\)
  • Use \(x=r\,\text{cos}\,θ,\) \(y=r\,\text{sin}\,θ,\) and \(dA=r\,dr\,dθ\) to convert an integral in rectangular coordinates to an integral in polar coordinates. (See Example 2 and Example 3.)
  • Use \({r}^{2}={x}^{2}+{y}^{2}\) and \(θ={\text{tan}}^{-1}(\frac{y}{x})\) to convert an integral in polar coordinates to an integral in rectangular coordinates, if needed. (See Example 8.)
  • To find the volume in polar coordinates bounded above by a surface \(z=f(r,θ)\) over a region on the \(xy\) -plane, use a double integral in polar coordinates. (See Example 6, Example 7, and Example 9.)
